And if you can't stand the idea of just eating a watermelon raw, or you lack the social breadth that's required to handle a whole lot of watermelon in one sitting, poke through the following recipes for one that seems a bit more practical.

  • How:

    Three kinds: How to determine ripeness, how to prepare, and how to enjoy.

    1. How to determine ripeness

      There's absolutely no point in bringing home a watermelon that doesn't actually smell like a watermelon. Your taste buds only tell you what something tastes like because your nose says it first. Take the time to determine whether your nose can pick out a ripe one - don't worry about being stared at; I've been there. Just know that if you hone the "sniff-out" skill, people will be lining up to ask you exactly how you know which watermelons are ripe. If you're in a grocery store where they wax their melons or where there's something that's smelling way stronger just a few feet away than the melon in your hands, here's one other thing that you can try: Flick your finger agains the side of the melon, as if flicking off an annoying insect. (Experts call this method "thumping.") If what responds is a hollow sound, you can bet it's ripe and ready to be eaten.

    2. How to clean and prepare

      The most you need to worry about for a watermelon is washing the outer rind. Sound ludicrous? Who knows what's been bumping up against it since it went from seed to you! And you're probably not going to wash your hands between the moment you hold a piece of watermelon in your hands to the next when you rub your eyes or lick your fingers. Once washed, the only other preparation is in selecting a large enough knife to clear your whole watermelon. I'm not picky, though I expect you to have no leftovers once cut. First determine what you'll be using your melon for and whether you want to be able to store any remainders in the fridge for later. If this is going to be the case, cut from one end in circular slices. If plastic-wrapped in the fridge later, the majority of the still-encased melon will stay fresh even if the part right up against the plastic doesn't.
